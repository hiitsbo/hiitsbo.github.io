Turning Accessibility Guidelines into Clear, Role‑Based Actions

Redesigned accessibility documentation for design system components to eliminate confusion and streamline implementation across teams.

Duration
August 2025 - Present
(Core components complete; method rolling out system-wide)
My Role
Design System Accessibility Lead
Stakeholders
UX Designers,
Software Engineers,
QA Engineers

Overview

As the Design System Accessibility Lead, I shape how accessibility is implemented through design reviews, manual audits, and component documentation.

Over time, I noticed a pattern: teams genuinely wanted to get accessibility right, but existing guidance wasn’t aligned with their workflows, leading to frustration across disciplines. To address this, I introduced a role-based, actionable documentation model that gives designers, developers, and QA engineers clear, immediate steps to implement accessibility correctly.

This case study focuses on the Button component to illustrate the approach; the method is now being applied across all components in the design system.

Problem

Teams wanted to do the right thing for accessibility, but the complexity of the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) left them unsure how to begin.

Designer

What they were told

“Please make sure your design is accessible and annotate accessibility in your Figma files.”

But they weren’t sure what “accessible” and “annotate” really required.

Things they often asked

  • Is this accessible?
  • What do I need to do to make it accessible?
  • Do I need to annotate anything for a simple button?
  • What exactly should I write in the annotation?

What actually happened

Either no annotations, or annotations that were not accurate, inconsistent and incomplete.

Developer

What they were told

“This component is accessible out of the box when implemented correctly.”

But they didn’t know where that guarantee stopped.

Things they often asked

  • What does this component handle by default for accessibility?
  • Which accessibility aspects are context-dependent and still my responsibility?
  • How do I make it accessible?

What actually happened

Either over-trusted the component (“it’s accessible out of the box, so I’m done”), or reached out repeatedly to clarify what they should still be doing.

QA Engineer

What they were told

“Please test this for accessibility and report any issues.”

But they didn’t know what to look for.

Things they often asked

  • What’s the expected behavior?
  • Am I supposed to test with keyboard only, or with screen reader too?
  • What exactly counts as an accessibility issue?
  • If I find something, do I go to the designer, the developer, or the accessibility team?

What actually happened

QA spent extra time guessing or asking around, and sometimes accessibility bugs weren’t reported or got stuck because it wasn’t clear who owned the fix.

Goal

Create one documentation pattern that:

  • Meets designers, developers, and QA in their existing workflows to make accessibility part of the process.
  • Translates abstract accessibility requirements into clear, role-specific actions.
  • Reduces the number of “What should I do?” emails and messages.

Solutions

One accessibility requirement.

Three role-specific instructions that work together to get it right.

I structured accessibility documentation with role-based entry points, so each discipline could quickly understand what they own—without duplicating or contradicting guidance.

Using WCAG 2.5.3 (Label in Name) as an example, accessibility requirements are translated into clear, role-specific guidance.

  1. Counter_1Clear triggers for annotation — Makes annotation expectations explicit, so designers know exactly when to contribute.
  2. Counter_2Context-specific guidance — Explains when extra context is needed vs. when visible labels are enough.
  3. Counter_3Visual Do/Don't examples — Reduces ambiguity by showing exactly what good annotations look like.
  1. Counter_1Cross-references design annotations — Connects developer work to designer decisions, reducing back-and-forth.
  2. Counter_2Clear decision tree — Guides developers to the right pattern and clarifies when no additional implementation is required, removing guesswork.
  3. Counter_3Code-level requirements — Translates accessibility needs into concrete props, ARIA attributes, and labeling rules so implementation doesn’t rely on inference.
  1. Counter_1Testable acceptance criteria — Written in WHEN/THEN format with plain-language steps so anyone (not just accessibility experts) can follow them and verify expected behavior.
  2. Counter_2Visual verification examples — Recordings show exactly what "passing" looks and sounds like.
  3. Counter_3Cross-references to requirements — Traceability tags link each test criterion to the relevant designer/developer considerations section, clarifying ownership and speeding fixes.

Next Steps

  • Implement this documentation approach across components to achieve full coverage.
  • Capture baseline and post-launch metrics (number of accessibility bugs, review comments, etc.) to quantify impact.
